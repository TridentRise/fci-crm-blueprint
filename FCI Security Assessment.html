Layer 2 — Network Penetration Testing & Vulnerability Scanning (Internal & External)

Network penetration testing and vulnerability scanning simulates real-world attacks against the firm's infrastructure to identify vulnerabilities before an attacker does. Traditionally, this testing focused on external-facing systems — testing from the outside in.

That is changing. Regulators are now requiring firms to test from the inside as well. Internal penetration testing evaluates what an attacker could do after gaining initial access — moving laterally through the network, escalating privileges, and reaching sensitive data.

Regulatory Landscape
Regulator Requirement Internal Testing
NYDFS — New York Department of Financial Services 23 NYCRR 500.5 requires annual penetration testing "from both inside and outside the information systems' boundaries." Effective April 29, 2024. Explicitly required
SEC — Securities and Exchange Commission Regulation S-P (updated 2024) requires written policies and procedures for safeguarding customer records and information. Penetration testing is expected as part of demonstrating compliance, though not prescribed by name. Expected, not mandated
FINRA — Financial Industry Regulatory Authority FINRA's Report on Selected Cybersecurity Practices recommends penetration testing, particularly for customer-facing systems and sensitive data stores. Annual cybersecurity risk assessments are expected. Recommended
NAIC — National Association of Insurance Commissioners The NAIC Insurance Data Security Model Law (adopted by 24+ states) requires annual assessment of safeguards' key controls, systems, and procedures. The model is flexible on method, but penetration testing is the accepted standard. Implied by model law

FCI performs both internal and external network penetration testing and vulnerability scanning. For firms subject to NYDFS — or firms that want to get ahead of where regulation is heading — internal testing is no longer optional.

Layer 3 — Cloud Application Hardening (Microsoft 365)

Microsoft 365 is where most firms store sensitive client data, communicate, and collaborate. It is the environment advisors and staff use every day — and it is a primary attack surface.

Cloud application hardening reviews the configuration of the firm's Microsoft 365 environment against security best practices. This includes email security settings, data sharing permissions, conditional access policies, and multi-factor authentication enforcement. Misconfigured settings create openings that attackers exploit — often without the firm knowing the exposure exists.

FCI assesses the current configuration, identifies gaps, and tightens the settings to reduce the attack surface.

Layer 4 — External Attack Surface Assessment

The external attack surface is everything visible from the outside — what an attacker or an automated scanning tool would see when looking at the firm. This includes public-facing servers, open ports, DNS records, email authentication settings, SSL certificates, and the firm's marketing and corporate website.

This layer is critical because cyber insurance carriers now use automated tools to verify a firm's external attack surface before underwriting or renewing a policy. A poor external posture can directly affect insurability and premium pricing.

The assessment identifies what is exposed, what should not be, and what needs to be corrected — before an insurer, attacker, or regulator finds it first.

Most firms that have gone through a security assessment received a report and nothing else. The vendor ran the scans, delivered the findings, and moved on. FCI's approach is different.

FCI gives the firm a defined window to address the findings. FCI's team is available during this period to help implement fixes and close gaps. The firm is not left to figure it out alone.

The final report demonstrates not just that issues were found, but that they were resolved. This turns the assessment from a point-in-time snapshot into a measurable improvement cycle — and gives the firm a document that proves progress, not just problems.
Section 3
Why Firms Run Security Assessments
There are four primary reasons a firm initiates a security assessment. Understanding the motivation shapes the scope, urgency, and audience for the results.
1. Self-Validation

The firm wants to know where it stands. Either it confirms that its security posture is solid — which provides confidence to leadership, the board, and clients — or it identifies weaknesses and builds the case for budget to address them. In both outcomes, the firm is better off than not knowing.

2. Regulatory Requirement

Regulators — the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA), the New York Department of Financial Services (NYDFS), and state insurance departments — ask for copies of security assessments during examinations. Having a current, comprehensive assessment ready is a compliance expectation. Firms that do not have one face harder questions and increased scrutiny.

3. Home Office Requirement

The home office, carrier, or enterprise above the sales office requires annual security assessments as part of its oversight. This can be a standing annual requirement — built into the affiliation agreement or compliance calendar — or triggered by enforcement after an incident or audit finding. In either case, the sales office needs to produce results on a defined timeline.

4. Mergers & Acquisitions

On the sell side, a firm wants to demonstrate the value and security maturity of what the buyer is acquiring. A strong assessment adds to the firm's credibility and negotiating position. On the buy side, the acquirer wants to understand and reduce liability. A poor security assessment can directly reduce the acquisition value — or introduce conditions and holdbacks into the deal.
