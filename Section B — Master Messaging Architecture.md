## Part 1 — Universal Messages

These lines apply regardless of persona. They establish what FCI is, what it does, and why it is different. Every asset uses at least one of these.

### The one-sentence positioning

FCI manages cybersecurity and compliance for financial firms that operate through distributed field models — so every advisor, every device, and every field office is covered, and every examiner gets the evidence they need.

### The three pillars

Every FCI asset should connect to at least one of these three ideas. They are the organizing logic for all messaging.

Pillar 1 — Enforcement without friction
Controls are set and maintained automatically, without touching your users' workflows. Your advisors do not notice the protections running. Your compliance team does not have to ask anyone to do anything differently.

Pillar 2 — Proof at exam time
Every safeguard FCI manages is logged, timestamped, and mapped to the framework your regulator expects. When the examiner walks in, the evidence is already assembled. The CISO Portal produces it in minutes.

Pillar 3 — Expert coverage, not just software
A human-led SOC watches every endpoint, 24 hours a day, seven days a week. When something needs a response, a person responds — not an automated alert that sits in a queue.

### The four proof points

These are the claims that underpin every pillar. Use them exactly as written. Do not round up, do not embellish.

- 40,000+ endpoints under management
- Up to 90% reduction in implementation time via the CISO Portal
- Up to 80% reduction in recurring monthly compliance management tasks
- 30+ years serving exclusively financial services firms — RIAs, broker-dealers, and insurance carriers

### Certifications and standards (use when credibility is needed)

- SOC 2 Type 1 certified
- 100% SecurityScorecard rating
- NIST Cybersecurity Framework mapped controls
- SEC, FINRA, NYDFS, and NAIC regulatory alignment

### The call to action (universal)

Request a Security Assessment. In 30 minutes, you will have a clear picture of your current exposure and a map of what your next regulatory exam or cyber insurance renewal will ask for.

## Part 2 — Persona 1: The RIA Principal

### Who they are

The owner or managing partner of a registered investment advisory firm. They may have anywhere from 3 advisors in one office to 80 advisors across multiple locations. They are personally responsible for compliance — in many firms, there is no separate CCO. They wear the compliance hat alongside the business development hat, and the weight of both is real.

### Their world

They built their practice to serve clients, not to manage technology. But every year the regulatory expectations grow — from cybersecurity policies to annual reviews to evidence of controls on every device their advisors use. Their advisors are often semi-independent, working from home offices and personal devices. A single breach, a single exam failure, or a single FINRA fine can permanently damage the trust they have spent years building.

### The job they need done

- Pass the next SEC or FINRA exam without spending weeks preparing evidence manually
- Know that every advisor's device is protected, regardless of where they work or what network they use
- Keep advisors focused on clients, not on security software, policy updates, or compliance checklists
- Have a credible answer when a cyber insurance carrier asks what controls are in place
- Sleep at night knowing that someone qualified is watching

### The fear that drives the decision

"A breach or an exam failure would destroy client trust that took me 20 years to build. And I would not even know it was happening until it was too late."

### FCI's answer to this persona

FCI quietly manages every device and control in the background — so your practice stays compliant, your advisors stay focused, and your next exam produces a report instead of a crisis.

### Persona-specific proof points

- NIST CSF-mapped controls enforced on every endpoint, including BYOD and firm-owned devices, without user involvement
- The CISO Portal produces exam-ready documentation in minutes — mapped to what SEC and FINRA examiners actually ask for
- Always-On VPN and MFA enforced automatically — advisors connect securely without extra steps
- Zero Trust access controls contain any incident before it can spread across the practice

### What changes on day one

On day one, FCI's team takes inventory of every device in the practice. Controls are pushed silently and automatically. The principal receives a dashboard showing the full posture of every endpoint. For most practices, the first view of that dashboard is the first time anyone has seen the complete picture.

### What they can show an auditor or insurer

- Full endpoint inventory with compliance status for every device
- Evidence log showing which controls are active, when they were applied, and whether any were tampered with
- SEC and FINRA-aligned policy documentation, timestamped
- MFA and VPN enforcement records for all users
- Incident response documentation if any event occurred

### The sentence that makes them lean in

"Your advisors will not notice the difference. Your examiner will."

## Part 3 — Persona 2: The Broker-Dealer Compliance Officer

### Who they are

The head of compliance, CCO, or supervision officer at a registered broker-dealer. They are responsible for the security and compliance posture of the firm, including every registered representative working out of independent or branch offices they do not directly control. They answer to regulators — FINRA, SEC, and potentially state securities regulators — and they answer to firm leadership when anything goes wrong.

### Their world

They supervise a network that is structurally difficult to control. Registered representatives use their own devices, operate in offices that may not have dedicated IT support, and connect through networks that no central team manages. FINRA's examination priorities and the SEC's cybersecurity rules demand evidence of specific controls — written supervisory procedures, vendor due diligence, access controls, incident response planning. Assembling that evidence manually, across a distributed network, takes their team weeks before every exam.

### The job they need done

- Demonstrate to FINRA and SEC examiners that cybersecurity controls are enforced across every branch and every rep, not just at the home office
- Reduce the time their team spends preparing exam documentation — they need evidence that assembles itself
- Prove that written supervisory procedures (WSPs) are backed by actual technical controls, not just policies on paper
- Get visibility into every device and connection in the network, including BYOD
- Be able to answer a FINRA examiner's cybersecurity questionnaire confidently and immediately

### The fear that drives the decision

"FINRA asks me to prove that our cybersecurity controls are working across our entire rep network. Right now, I know they are not — and I cannot prove they are."

### FCI's answer to this persona

FCI enforces controls across your entire rep network — home offices, branch offices, BYOD devices — and produces the FINRA and SEC-aligned evidence your examiners expect, before they walk in the door.

### Persona-specific proof points

- NIST CSF controls mapped specifically to FINRA's examination priorities and SEC Regulation S-P requirements
- The CISO Portal provides a single view of every device in the rep network — compliance status, control enforcement, and incident history
- Written supervisory procedure templates aligned to current SEC and FINRA cybersecurity expectations
- Vendor due diligence documentation produced as part of the FCI engagement — FCI itself is the evidence of a vetted cybersecurity vendor

### What changes on day one

On day one, the compliance officer gets a complete inventory of the firm's distributed device landscape — many firms discover devices and connections they did not know existed. Controls are enforced and logged from that point forward. The exam evidence gap begins closing immediately.

### What they can show an auditor or insurer

- Device inventory and control enforcement log across all reps and branches
- FINRA and SEC cybersecurity rule alignment mapping
- Written supervisory procedure documentation backed by technical evidence
- Vendor due diligence file for FCI (SOC 2 Type 1, SecurityScorecard, regulatory alignment)
- Incident log and response documentation
- MFA, VPN, and endpoint encryption enforcement records

### The sentence that makes them lean in

"The FINRA examiner will ask for your cybersecurity evidence. FCI builds it while your reps are working — not after the exam notice arrives."

## Part 4 — Persona 3: The Insurance Carrier or Agency Operations Leader

### Who they are

An operations executive, compliance officer, or technology leader at an insurance carrier or independent insurance agency network. They are responsible for the compliance and security posture of a business that runs partly or entirely through a field agency model — independent agents and brokers working from their own offices, on their own devices, under the carrier's or network's regulatory umbrella.

### Their world

The insurance sector faces converging pressure from three directions. State regulators — particularly under NAIC Model Law requirements — are expanding cybersecurity mandates for carriers and the agents they appoint. Cyber insurance carriers are requiring proof of specific controls at policy renewal, and some are declining coverage for firms that cannot demonstrate them. And the distributed agency model creates the same visibility problem that broker-dealers face: security controls that exist at the home office do not reach the field, and the field is where most of the customer data lives.

### The job they need done

- Meet NAIC Model Law cybersecurity requirements without building a large internal security team
- Produce the control evidence that cyber insurance carriers request at renewal — before the renewal conversation, not during it
- Extend security controls to appointed agents and independent field offices who are outside the firm's direct IT management
- Demonstrate to state regulators that the firm's cybersecurity program reaches every part of the distribution network
- Reduce the cost and complexity of managing compliance across a large, distributed agent population

### The fear that drives the decision

"We had a breach in an independent agency, and our cyber insurance carrier is asking why our controls did not extend to that office. We do not have a good answer."

### FCI's answer to this persona

FCI extends your cybersecurity and compliance controls to every appointed agent and field office — so your NAIC filings reflect what is actually happening in the field, and your cyber insurer sees the evidence they need to renew your policy.

### Persona-specific proof points

- NAIC Model Law-aligned cybersecurity controls deployed to independent agents and field offices without requiring them to manage anything themselves
- Cyber insurance documentation package produced by the CISO Portal — exactly the evidence carriers ask for at renewal
- Zero Trust access controls ensure that a compromise in one field agency cannot reach carrier systems or other agents
- 24x7 SOC monitoring extends across the full agent network — not just the home office

### What changes on day one

On day one, FCI maps the full scope of the agency network — appointed agents, their devices, and their connections to carrier systems. The operations leader often sees, for the first time, the full scope of exposure that the field model creates. Controls begin deploying immediately. The NAIC compliance posture improves from the first week.

### What they can show a regulator or insurer

- NAIC Model Law cybersecurity program documentation
- Appointed agent device inventory and control enforcement log
- Cyber insurance renewal package — control evidence mapped to the carrier's specific questionnaire format
- Zero Trust access control records showing which agents and devices can reach which systems
- Incident and response log for the full field network
- Annual cybersecurity review documentation required under state law

### The sentence that makes them lean in

"Your cyber insurer will ask what controls you had in place. FCI makes sure you have an answer before they ask."

## Part 5 — Shared Objection Responses

These are the four objections FCI hears most often, across all personas. Responses should be used in conversations, FAQ documents, and email sequences — always in a tone that acknowledges the concern before addressing it.

### "We already have an IT provider."

An IT provider manages performance and uptime. FCI manages the compliance posture and security controls that regulators and cyber insurers actually ask about. Most IT providers do not produce NIST-mapped evidence, do not run a 24x7 SOC, and are not built around the distributed field model your firm runs on. FCI works alongside your IT provider — the two roles do not overlap.

### "We are too small for this level of security."

Regulators and cyber insurers do not adjust their expectations based on firm size. A 10-person RIA faces the same SEC cybersecurity examination questions as a 500-person broker-dealer. FCI is designed for exactly this — providing enterprise-level compliance coverage at a scale and cost that smaller financial firms can actually sustain.

### "We have never had an incident, so we are probably fine."

Most firms that experience a breach had never had one before. The distributed field model — BYOD, home networks, independent offices — creates exposure that does not announce itself until it is too late. The question regulators and insurers ask is not "have you been breached?" but "can you prove your controls were in place?" FCI answers that second question.

### "We will look at this after our next exam."

Exam preparation should not start when the notice arrives. The evidence FCI produces is built continuously — every day controls are enforced and logged. Firms that start after the notice often spend weeks assembling documentation that FCI-managed firms already have ready. The firms that fare best in examinations started preparing before the examiner was scheduled.

## Part 6 — Vocabulary Reference

Use these terms consistently across all FCI materials. Inconsistency in terminology creates doubt about expertise.

Use: safeguards, controls, enforcement, evidence, posture, exam-ready, field compliance, distributed, BYOD, continuous monitoring, Zero Trust, endpoint visibility, MXDR, SOC

Do not use: solutions (vague), suite (dated), platform (overused in the category), cutting-edge, next-generation, best-of-breed, seamless, robust

Regulatory bodies: always spell out on first use — Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA), New York Department of Financial Services (NYDFS), National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) — then use the abbreviation thereafter.

Frameworks: NIST Cybersecurity Framework (NIST CSF), Center for Internet Security Controls (CIS Controls), Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA). Always explain the business benefit alongside the framework name — never cite a framework without stating what it means for the client.
