05 — Voice & Tone

How FCI sounds

Every sentence should answer "so what?" before it is published. Lead with the client's world, not FCI's capabilities. Short sentences. One idea per sentence. Outcomes, not features.

Use
Short declarative sentences. One idea at a time.
Outcome verbs: enforcing, proving, reducing, demonstrating, preventing, simplifying.
Hedged claims: "helps," "supports," "enables," "provides evidence of."
Specific numbers over vague adjectives — "40,000 endpoints" rather than "thousands of clients."
Client's problem first, FCI's solution second. Always.
Named frameworks — SEC, FINRA, NYDFS, NIST CSF — when they help the reader take action.
Avoid
Superlatives without proof: "best-in-class," "world-class," "cutting-edge."
Meaningless adjectives: "robust," "holistic," "seamless," "comprehensive."
Absolute security claims. No promises of zero breaches or guaranteed protection.
Bold text in prose. Write clearly enough that no emphasis is needed.
Scare tactics. Describe risk plainly — do not amplify fear to close a deal.
Jargon-first language. Lead in plain English; acronyms follow only when useful.
06 — Imagery Direction

What FCI looks like

Photography should reflect the professional world of FCI's clients — calm, light, and human. The subject is always a person in a real work context. No clichés, no dark drama.

Use

Use
Compliance team reviewing a report — quiet concentration, professional context
Use
Branch office or field setting — distributed, professional, human scale
Avoid
Dark rooms, green terminal text, hooded figures — hacker clichés that signal generic security
Avoid
Abstract dark gradients, lock icons, floating shields, neural network graphics
Avoid
3D charts, excessive drop shadows, and data-center stock photography
