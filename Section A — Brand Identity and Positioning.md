# Section A — Brand Identity and Positioning
### FCI Rebranding Project | Working Document

---

## 1. The Brand Name

FCI. That is it.

No tagline appended to the name itself. No "FCI Cyber" or "FCI Security." Three letters that carry 30 years of earned credibility in one specific market. The goal is for "FCI" to function the way "IBM" or "ADP" or "TIAA" function in the minds of financial professionals — a proper noun that signals authority, not an abbreviation that needs explaining.

Every asset leads with FCI in the logo mark. The descriptor line follows below, at smaller scale, in a lighter weight.

---

## 2. The Two-Layer Tagline System

### Layer 1 — The descriptor line (goes under the logo)

> Managed Cybersecurity for Financial Services

This line does exactly what it says. It tells a prospect immediately what FCI is and who it serves. It belongs on the logo lockup, email signatures, business cards, the website header, and any context where someone encounters FCI for the first time.

Why this works: it is clear, it is not clever, and clarity wins in a regulated industry where buyers are already confused by too many vendors making too many promises.

### Layer 2 — The brand promise line (used in headlines, intros, presentations, one-pagers)

This is the line that differentiates. It does not appear under the logo. It appears where FCI is making a case — in a conversation, on a website hero, at the top of a one-pager, in the first line of a sales email.

> "Built for the way financial firms actually work."

This line speaks directly to the problem that generic MSSPs ignore: financial services does not operate like a centralized enterprise. It operates through field offices, independent advisors, distributed agency networks, and BYOD devices that no IT team controls. FCI is the only managed cybersecurity firm that was built for that model, not retrofitted to it.

That is the differentiation. That is the sentence that makes a CCO or an RIA principal nod before you finish saying it.

### How the two layers work together

In practice, a first touch might read:

> FCI — Managed Cybersecurity for Financial Services
> Built for the way financial firms actually work.

The first line tells them what you are. The second line tells them why you are different. Together, they give a new prospect enough to lean in.

## 3. The Core Positioning Statement

For internal use — the single paragraph that every writer, designer, and salesperson should memorize before producing anything for FCI.

> FCI is the managed cybersecurity and compliance partner for financial firms that operate through distributed field models — independent advisors, field insurance agencies, and branch offices. We enforce the safeguards, generate the audit evidence, and maintain the continuous coverage that regulators and cyber insurers expect. We have done this exclusively for financial services firms for more than 30 years. That focus is not a feature. It is the product.

## 4. Logo Direction

### The mark

FCI — set in three uppercase letters, no icon, no emblem, no shield, no abstract graphic.

The letters carry the brand. The discipline to leave everything else out is what signals confidence.

### Typeface

Primary recommendation: a geometric sans-serif at regular or medium weight.

Candidates to brief a designer with:

- Neue Haas Grotesk — authoritative, precise, Swiss-influenced. The choice if FCI wants to project institutional depth alongside modernity.
- Söhne (by Klim) — warmer version of the same tradition, slightly more approachable. Good if FCI wants "trusted partner" over "enterprise authority."
- Inter — open source, widely available, highly legible at small sizes. A practical choice if FCI needs a typeface that works across web, print, and client deliverables without licensing friction.
- DM Sans — clean, modern, slightly rounder corners. The choice if FCI wants to feel approachable without sacrificing professionalism.

Recommendation: Neue Haas Grotesk for the logo mark, Inter for the body system (website, documents, slides). This gives FCI a high-end logo presence and a practical, accessible system for everyday production.

### Letter spacing

Slightly wider than default — tracking of approximately +20 to +40 units on the FCI mark. This creates breathing room between the letters, reinforces the minimalist feel, and ensures the mark scales clearly at all sizes from a browser favicon to a trade-show backdrop.

### Weight

Medium weight for the logo mark. Not bold — bold collapses into heaviness at small sizes and conflicts with the minimalist intent. Not light — light at small sizes loses presence. Medium holds both.

### Capitalization

Always uppercase for the FCI mark. The descriptor line ("Managed Cybersecurity for Financial Services") in sentence case or all-lowercase — to be decided in the first logo concepts, but lowercase creates a useful visual contrast between the authority of FCI and the plain-language of the descriptor.

### Clear space

A clear space equal to the cap-height of the letter C on all four sides of the logo mark. Nothing lives in that space — no other text, no graphic elements.

## 5. Color System

### Primary palette

Deep Navy — the authority color. Used for backgrounds, primary text on light surfaces, and any context requiring gravitas.
- Suggested value: #0F1F3D or #112240
- Think: the Bloomberg terminal. The financial statements. The audit report cover.

Electric Blue — the energy color. Used for CTAs, highlights, links, active states, and the moments where FCI signals confidence rather than weight.
- Suggested value: #2563EB or #1D4ED8
- Think: a live dashboard indicator. A compliance status showing green. A monitoring signal that is on and running.

### Secondary palette

Cool Off-White — the breathing color. Used for page backgrounds, card surfaces, and negative space.
- Suggested value: #F8FAFC or #F1F5F9
- This is not white. It is slightly cool, slightly blue-tinted. It reinforces the palette without adding another named color.

Slate Gray — the supporting text color. Used for body copy, captions, and secondary information.
- Suggested value: #475569 or #64748B
- Never pure black for body text. The blue-gray undertone keeps everything in the same color family.

### Accent

One accent color, used sparingly. The purpose of an accent is to signal action and alert — not to decorate.
- Suggested value: a brighter teal, approximately #0EA5E9 or #06B6D4
- Use for: notification badges, compliance status indicators, key data points in charts, and hover states on buttons

### What this palette says

A prospect opening an FCI document should feel the same way they feel opening a Goldman Sachs report or a Vanguard fund prospectus — calm, serious, precise. Not excited. Not alarmed. Confident. The color system does that work before a single word is read.

### What this palette avoids

Red: the old brand. Also associated with danger and error — never the primary color for a firm asking clients to trust them with their compliance posture.
Green: used universally by MSPs and IT vendors — it no longer signals security, it signals commodity.
Orange/Yellow: energy without authority — works for consumer brands, not for a firm managing regulatory risk.
Dark teal as primary: trendy, will date quickly, and used by too many current-era cybersecurity firms.

## 6. Typography System

### For the logo

Neue Haas Grotesk Medium, uppercase, tracked wide — the mark.
Inter Regular, sentence case — the descriptor line.

### For documents and slides

Headlines: Inter, weight 600 (SemiBold), large size, generous line height
Subheadings: Inter, weight 500 (Medium), slightly smaller
Body: Inter, weight 400 (Regular), 16px / 1.6 line height on screen, equivalent in print
Data and metrics callouts: Inter Mono or Roboto Mono, weight 400 — for dashboard values, compliance scores, percentage claims
Captions and labels: Inter, weight 400, small size, slate gray color

### Type scale principle

Use fewer sizes, used boldly. A page with one very large headline and clean body text reads more confidently than a page with six different sizes competing for attention. Let the scale do the hierarchy work, not the color or the weight.

## 7. Visual Language Rules

### What to use

- Wide margins. Every page, every slide, every document section should have more white space than feels comfortable at first. That discomfort is the right instinct — lean into it.
- Photography of real environments: advisor at a desk, a compliance team reviewing a report, a branch office setting. The subject is always a person in a professional context, not a screen, not a data center, not a padlock.
- Data visualization that looks like it belongs in a financial report — clean axis lines, minimal color, labeled clearly. Never three-dimensional chart effects.
- The occasional single-line accent — a thin horizontal rule in Electric Blue — to separate sections without adding visual weight.

### What to avoid

- Hacker iconography: dark rooms, green matrix text, skull-and-crossbones, hooded figures. FCI serves the compliance officer, not the pen tester.
- Abstract security imagery: blue globes, lock icons, shield graphics, neural network visuals. These are invisible in the category — every vendor uses them.
- Stock photo smiles: the handshake photo, the team-around-a-laptop photo, the person-pointing-at-a-chart photo. These reduce trust, not build it.
- Drop shadows, gradients, and 3D elements. They age badly and conflict with the Zen direction.
- Clutter. If a layout has 10 elements, remove 4 and the remaining 6 will be more powerful.

## 8. Voice in One Page

The brand voice is the written version of the visual identity — restrained, precise, client-first.

Every sentence should be answerable with "so what?" before it is published. If the answer is "so what?" and the text doesn't answer it, rewrite.

Short sentences. One idea per sentence. Never two clauses when one will do.

Lead with the client's world, not FCI's capabilities. The client does not care what FCI does — they care what changes for them. Start there.

Use these verbs: enforcing, proving, reducing, demonstrating, preventing, simplifying.

Avoid these words and phrases: "best-in-class," "cutting-edge," "holistic," "robust," "seamlessly," "leverage," "synergy," "world-class." These words mean nothing. Replace them with a specific number or a specific outcome.

No absolute security claims. No promises of zero breaches. No language that implies FCI can prevent all incidents. Use: "helps," "supports," "enables," "provides evidence of," "reduces exposure to."

No bold text in prose. Bold is a crutch for underlining what you are not confident the reader will find themselves. Write clearly enough that no bolding is needed.

## 9. What This Means for Every Channel

Website hero: "FCI" as the mark, "Managed Cybersecurity for Financial Services" as the descriptor, "Built for the way financial firms actually work." as the first body line. Single CTA: Request a Security Assessment.

One-pager headline: starts with the client's problem, not FCI's name.

Presentation cover: FCI mark, descriptor, client name, date. Nothing else on the cover slide.

Email signature: Name, title, FCI — Managed Cybersecurity for Financial Services, phone, website. No graphics, no banner ads, no compliance disclaimers (unless legally required).

LinkedIn: Written in first person from FCI leadership. Practical observations about regulatory developments, compliance exam trends, and the operational reality of distributed financial firms. No product promotions in organic posts.
