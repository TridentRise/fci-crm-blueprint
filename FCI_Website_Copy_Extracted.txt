Know exactly where your firm stands. Before your regulator asks.
FCI's Security Assessment is a layered, four-part evaluation of your firm's cybersecurity posture — covering controls, vulnerabilities, cloud configuration, and external exposure. In 60 to 80 percent of assessments, we find compliance gaps the firm did not know existed.
The Four Layers
Layer 1 — Gap Analysis
What controls are in place — and can the firm prove it?
FCI evaluates the firm's security posture across six domains: Firm, Endpoint, User, Network, Cloud App, and Data. For each, we assess what controls exist and whether the firm has the evidence to prove it — the evidence a regulator, insurer, or home office would require. In 30 minutes, the firm knows exactly where the gaps are.
Layer 2 — Network Pen Testing & Vulnerability Scanning
Can someone break in? What exposures exist right now?
Penetration testing simulates real-world attacks against the firm's infrastructure — both from outside and from inside the network. Vulnerability scanning systematically identifies known weaknesses: missing patches, misconfigured services, open ports. Together, they give the firm a real-world stress test and a prioritized inventory of risk. FCI performs both internal and external testing — internal testing is now required by NYDFS and expected by most regulators.
Layer 3 — Cloud App Hardening
Is your Microsoft 365 environment configured securely?
Microsoft 365 is where most firms store sensitive client data and communicate daily — and it is a primary attack surface. FCI reviews email security, data sharing permissions, conditional access policies, and MFA enforcement against best practices. The average organization's Microsoft Secure Score is between 30% and 45%. Most firms do not know their number.
Layer 4 — External Attack Surface
What can an attacker — or an insurer — see from the outside?
This layer scans everything visible from outside the firm: public-facing servers, open ports, DNS records, email authentication, SSL certificates, and the corporate website. Cyber insurance carriers now use automated tools to verify a firm's external posture before underwriting or renewing a policy. A poor result can directly affect insurability and premium pricing.

Zero Trust
Explains Zero Trust in plain language for financial firm executives. Focuses on containing field exposure — not technical architecture.
Verify every user, device, and network. Before they reach anything.
Zero Trust is not a product. It is a principle: nothing on your network is trusted by default. Every access request is verified — user identity, device health, location, and context — before a connection is allowed. FCI deploys this model pragmatically for firms with distributed field operations.
Why This Matters for Your Firm
When one device is compromised, Zero Trust determines how far the damage spreads.
In a traditional network model, a compromised device in a branch office or field agency has access to everything that device user normally reaches. Client data. Internal systems. Other network connections.
In a Zero Trust model, that same device — once flagged — can reach nothing it has not been explicitly authorized to access. The blast radius of any incident is contained at the source.
For financial firms with dozens or hundreds of field locations, Zero Trust is not a luxury. It is the architecture that makes distributed operations defensible.
How FCI Deploys Zero Trust
Always-On VPN
Every advisor and rep session runs through FCI's managed VPN — automatically, without requiring the user to connect manually. Every session is encrypted. Every connection is logged. Regulators see consistent, verifiable evidence of encrypted access across your entire network.
Identity Verification
Multi-factor authentication enforced on every device and every access point. Not optional. Not user-configured. Deployed and maintained by FCI — consistently, without drift.
Device Trust
Every device requesting access to firm systems is verified against a current health baseline — encryption status, patch level, endpoint protection active. Devices that fall out of compliance are flagged before they create exposure.
Access Segmentation
Access is granted by role, office, and supervisor — mapped from your directory services. Field advisors reach what they need. They cannot reach what they don't. A compromise in one location stays in one location.
