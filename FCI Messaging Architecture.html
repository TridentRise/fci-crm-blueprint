The owners, managing partners, and senior executives of financial services firms. Personally accountable for the firm's regulatory standing, client relationships, and survival. This role exists across all three markets — RIAs, broker-dealers & branch offices, and insurance carriers & agencies.

The person who built the firm or was hired to protect it — and who now understands that a single cyber event, not a market correction or a regulatory fine, is what could take it down.
Their world

A breach does not just disrupt operations. It triggers mandatory regulatory reporting, exposes client data, and creates liability that can destroy a firm built over decades. The pressure arrives from every direction at once: examinations are more detailed, cyber insurers demand proof of specific controls before renewing policies or paying a claim, and home offices require documentation that every affiliated sales office is in good order. They know they need coverage. What they often cannot confirm is that the controls are actually in place — across every user, every device, every network, and every cloud app.

CISO & CCO
The Chief Information Security Officer, Chief Compliance Officer, or senior compliance leader at the firm level. Responsible for defining the cybersecurity and compliance program and confirming that those requirements are met across the entire organization — including every sales office, branch, agency, and affiliated location. They answer to regulators. They answer to the board. When something goes wrong in the field, they answer for it.
Who they are
The person who sets the requirements — and who cannot currently prove those requirements are being followed in the field.
Their world
They set the program. Enforcement of that program depends on sales offices and field teams the home office does not directly control. Those offices may not have dedicated IT support. Their devices are often personal (BYOD). Their networks are outside the firm's perimeter. Regulators increasingly ask not just whether written policies exist, but whether those policies are backed by technical controls that can be verified. Assembling that verification — across dozens or hundreds of distributed offices — is a manual, time-consuming process that creates a gap between what the firm's policies say and what the firm can actually prove. Home offices are beginning to gain visibility into field devices — and what that visibility is revealing is a gap that compliance leadership is now expected to close.
The job they need done

Part 6 — Market
RIAs
Market context for RIA audiences. Lead with the role message from Parts 2–5 first. Use this section to add the regulatory environment and structural context specific to registered investment advisors and their affiliated advisor networks.
Who they are
Registered investment advisors are firms registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) or state securities regulators to provide investment advice for compensation. They range from solo practitioners to multi-billion dollar enterprises with dozens of affiliated advisors across multiple locations. RIAs operate under a fiduciary standard — legally required to act in the best interest of their clients. That relationship is built on trust, and it is the most valuable asset an RIA firm owns. A cybersecurity breach does not just create regulatory exposure. It erodes the client trust the entire business depends on.
How they operate
Many RIAs operate through a model where advisors work from their own offices, using their own devices, on networks the firm does not manage. Client data flows through personal laptops, email accounts, and cloud applications that may or may not meet regulatory standards. The firm's compliance officer — who may also be its managing partner — is responsible for confirming that cybersecurity controls extend to every one of those environments.
The regulatory environment
RIAs are examined by the SEC or state securities regulators. SEC examination priorities increasingly include cybersecurity — specifically evidence of controls rather than descriptions of policies. The SEC's cybersecurity rules require written policies, periodic risk assessments, and documentation upon examination. Cyber insurers require proof of specific controls — MFA, endpoint protection, access controls, incident response plans — before renewing policies. A smaller RIA faces the same documentation requirements as a larger one. The SEC and the cyber insurer ask the same questions regardless of firm size.
The specific challenge of the RIA model
The compliance obligation extends to every advisor and every device. But those advisors are often semi-independent, working from locations and on devices outside the firm's direct control. FCI was designed for exactly this environment — where the compliance requirement reaches to the field edge but where the firm has neither the staff nor the budget to manage a traditional security program.
FCI's answer for this market
FCI extends cybersecurity controls to every affiliated advisor — regardless of location, device ownership, or technical expertise — and produces the SEC-aligned documentation that examiners and cyber insurers require, continuously and automatically.
Part 7 — Market
Broker-Dealers & Branches
Market context for broker-dealer audiences. Lead with the role message from Parts 2–5 first. Use this section to add the regulatory environment and structural context specific to broker-dealers and their branch networks.
Who they are
Broker-dealers are firms registered with the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) and the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) to buy and sell securities on behalf of clients or for their own accounts. They operate through a network of registered representatives who may work out of the firm's owned branches or through independent offices affiliated with the firm but not directly owned by it. They range from large national firms with thousands of representatives to mid-size regional firms with dozens of branches and hundreds of reps.
How they operate
The broker-dealer home office sets the compliance program — the written supervisory procedures, cybersecurity policies, vendor oversight requirements, and examination protocols. That program must reach every branch and every registered representative, including those working from independent offices or home locations. Each branch may have its own technology environment, its own devices, and its own IT support or lack of it. The home office compliance team is responsible for proving that controls are in place across this entire distributed network — a network they do not directly manage.
The regulatory environment
FINRA's examination program has expanded its cybersecurity focus significantly. Examiners now routinely ask for evidence of specific controls: endpoint protection across all registered representative devices, MFA enforcement, access controls, incident response plans, vendor due diligence documentation, and written supervisory procedures backed by technical evidence — not just policies on paper. The SEC's Regulation S-P imposes specific requirements on broker-dealers related to the safeguarding of customer records. Examination findings increasingly cite the gap between a written policy and a technically enforced control. Findings from controls that failed to reach branch offices are among the most difficult to remediate.
The specific challenge of the branch model
Compliance officers need technical evidence that controls are working in offices they may never visit, on devices they do not own, managed by reps who are not cybersecurity professionals. FCI closes that gap — producing the FINRA and SEC-aligned documentation that examiners ask for, built continuously, across the full branch network.
FCI's answer for this market
FCI enforces controls across the full branch network — every registered representative, every device, every office — and produces FINRA and SEC-aligned evidence continuously, so the home office can confirm every branch is in good order before the examiner asks.
Part 8 — Market
Insurance Carriers & Agencies
Market context for insurance audiences. Lead with the role message from Parts 2–5 first. Use this section to add the regulatory environment and structural context specific to insurance carriers and their agency networks.
Who they are
Insurance carriers are companies licensed by state regulators and governed by the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) to underwrite insurance products. Independent insurance agencies are firms that distribute those products on behalf of one or more carriers, typically through networks of appointed agents who may work independently. The relationship between a carrier and its appointed agents creates a structural compliance challenge similar to the one broker-dealers face with their branches — the home office defines the requirements, and the field carries the risk.
How they operate
Insurance carriers distribute their products through appointed agents and independent agencies. Those agents hold private client data — health information, financial records, beneficiary designations — and connect to carrier systems to submit applications, access policy information, and process claims. The carrier is responsible for demonstrating that cybersecurity controls reach those agents, even though it does not own their offices or manage their technology.
The regulatory environment
The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) Model Data Security Law has been adopted by most states. It requires insurance carriers and large producers to develop and maintain a cybersecurity program, conduct risk assessments, oversee the security practices of third-party service providers, and notify regulators of cybersecurity events. State regulators are conducting more frequent and more detailed cybersecurity examinations of carriers, with increasing scope into their distribution networks. Cyber insurance compounds the challenge — carriers are being asked by their own cyber insurers for the same documentation they require their agents to maintain.
The specific challenge of the agency model
Many agencies operate as small businesses without IT staff. The compliance requirements flowing down from the carrier are legitimate. The means to meet them are often unclear. FCI extends cybersecurity controls to appointed agents and independent agencies without requiring those agents to become technology managers — so the carrier gets the documentation it needs, and the agent gets coverage without building a program from scratch.
FCI's answer for this market
FCI extends cybersecurity controls to every appointed agent and independent agency in the carrier's distribution network — and produces NAIC-aligned evidence that satisfies both the carrier's compliance requirements and the agent's cyber insurance obligations.
Part 9
Objection Responses
The four objections FCI hears most often, across all roles and markets. Always acknowledge the concern before addressing it. Use in conversations, FAQ sections, and email sequences.
We already have an IT provider.
An IT provider manages performance and uptime. FCI manages the compliance posture and security controls that regulators and cyber insurers actually examine. Most IT providers do not produce NIST-mapped evidence, do not run a 24x7 SOC, and are not built around the specific requirements of financial services sales offices. FCI works alongside the IT provider — the two roles do not overlap. The more important distinction: FCI's knowledge comes from managing cybersecurity across 40,000+ endpoints in financial services, through hundreds of regulatory examinations and cyber incidents. An IT provider serving multiple industries does not have that accumulated, sector-specific experience.
We are too small for this level of security.
Regulators and cyber insurers do not adjust their requirements based on firm size. A 12-person RIA faces the same SEC cybersecurity examination questions as a 500-person broker-dealer. A small insurance agency faces the same NAIC requirements as a large regional carrier. FCI is designed for exactly this situation — providing the controls, documentation, and expert oversight that regulators require, at a scale and cost that smaller financial services firms can sustain. The requirements do not scale down. FCI helps firms meet them regardless of size.
We have never had an incident, so we are probably fine.
Most firms that experience a breach had never had one before. The distributed model — personal devices, home networks, independent offices — creates exposure that does not announce itself. The question regulators, home offices, and cyber insurers ask is not whether a firm has been breached. It is whether the firm can prove its controls were in place. FCI answers that second question, continuously, before anyone asks it.
We will look at this after our next exam.
Examination preparation should not start when the notice arrives. The evidence FCI produces is built daily — every time controls are enforced and logged. Firms that start after the notice typically spend weeks assembling documentation that FCI-managed firms already have ready. The firms that fare best in examinations started preparing before the examiner was scheduled.

Always spell out on first use in any document, then use the abbreviation thereafter. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA). New York Department of Financial Services (NYDFS). National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC). NIST Cybersecurity Framework (NIST CSF). Never cite a framework name without stating what it means for the client.
